There's often no clear boundary to what constitutes content moderation. Users can't always tell if their content is compliant. When posts are deleted, they often don't know exactly why, whether the content was removed, hidden, or promoted. Therefore, people fabricate various explanations. Many describe content moderation as "shadow banning," biased algorithms, or unfair censorship. Others believe platforms secretly favor certain political views, creators, or trends. Even without evidence, these claims spread quickly because moderation systems are often invisible and difficult to understand. Consequently, users try various techniques to bypass content moderation, such as replacing words with homophones, changing spellings, or posting content at specific times to avoid censorship.

This phenomenon reflects a significant power imbalance between users and platforms. Platforms control rules, algorithms, and content visibility, while users have no way of knowing the criteria used by the platform to determine content violations. Most moderation systems are opaque, meaning users cannot fully understand or question them. Therefore, users are forced to rely on rumors, shared experiences, and community knowledge rather than official explanations. Platforms are increasingly becoming a powerful authority, capable of shaping speech, visibility, and even online identity. They can arbitrarily restrict the spread of information by controlling post content and push notifications. This represents the platform's immense power, while users are left with no choice but to passively accept it.

These findings can be linked to anthropological phenomena such as belief, resistance, and ritual. Belief manifests in users trusting and spreading stories about algorithms, even in the absence of evidence. Some myths about content moderation border on conspiracy theories because they explain hidden power structures. Resistance is reflected in users deliberately using code words or alternative spellings to circumvent moderation rules. Ritual is also manifested in repetitive online behaviors, such as carefully editing post content, checking interaction statistics, and repeatedly modifying and attempting to re-upload flagged posts. In short, folklore about content moderation reflects how people understand invisible digital power structures in their daily online lives.
